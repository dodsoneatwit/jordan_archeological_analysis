Intro to Jordan's Artifacts

Artifacts, the remains of past human activity, may include, but are not limited to ceramics, bones, and sculptures. Many artifacts like these can be traced back for thousands of years in many regions. One of these regions being Jordan, have shown great development in architecture, agriculture, and more over time that has allowed them to leave behind these types of findings for archaeologists to discover. In this case, sculptures, mosaics, and coins have been the most impactful discovery so far, and throughout this page, you will become aware of some of these artifacts such as the Apollo Sculpture, the Madaba Mosaic Map, and the Hand of Hercules.

Jordan Sculptures and Statues

The Hand of Hercules

At the Amman Citadel is the Hand of Hercules. Surrounded by Roman ruins, this statue can be seen broken into many pieces. Only three fingers of Hercules hand were found but once one has seen the size of only one finger, one can only imagine how huge the original statue was. It is said that the statue may have stood about 12m tall, but with only part of Hercules hand left, we will never know.

Tyche the Goddess of Amman

Tyche was the daughter of Zeus and Aphrodite. In her sculpture, only her head and a head piece of some sort can be found on the art. This piece was found in 1957 during some excavations and now it can be seen in the Jordan Archaeological Museum. According to history, Tyche brought fortune and prosperity to many cities within the region during the Hellenistic Period, while also protecting the region from incoming attacks.

Zeus the King of Olympus

Sculptures of both godly figures can be at the Jerash Museum. When introduced to Greek mythology, one of the first gods you hear of is Zeus, the god of sky and thunder. His sculpture encapsulates his divine power and might, even though it is slightly broken. The long hair, the bushed beard and the striking pose all give off a godly energy like no other.

The Beauty of Apollo

Seen as one of the most beautiful gods, Apollo represents truth, healing, music, poetry, and archery. He is also known as one of the most significant gods within Greek and Roman mythology. Apollo’s sculpture’s can be found in many temples around the world, but this sculpture itself represents a form of Greek sculptures that would present the beauty of youth through men (this is also called a Kouros).

Although Jordan’s currency nowadays is called Dinars, Jordanian coins back then came in many forms, with many older ones displaying different types of faces that some may not know of. Below are some of these copper coins, especially some from the Hellenistic Period

Hellenistic Coins

The Hellenistic Period utilized the knowledge of metals to create metal coins to use as currency back then. This became common in Jordan after they were invented around 700 B.C.E.. in Turkey. One of the first faces on these coins was Alexander the Great. The way Alexander changed the world was viewed as truly divine. His acts presented himself as some sort of demi-god to others and the way he lived his life so adventurously is what granted his face to be seen on these Hellenistic Coins.

Mosaics

Madaba is known for containing the largest number of mosaics that were discovered in their original location in the world. Mosaics are created from an art technique that uses a plethora of tiny parts to create a much larger work of art. Such a location houses a number of very unique and colorful mosaics that can be found all over the region. Moreover, each mosaic may tell a story or hold some type of symbolism that many archaeologists are eager to figure out. The most significant mosaic within Madaba can be found in St. George's Church and is named, the Madaba Mosaic Map.
This map dates back to the 6th century while and gives us an idea of what the Holy Land looked like during the Byzantine Period. Such a piece contains over two million pieces of colored in stone that depicts how the Nile Delta and Jerusalem looked back then. Many other mosaics can be found at other various sites such as the Archaeological Park I that houses a beautiful mosaic from the Church of the Virgin Mary and the Archaeological Museum that holds the earliest known mosaic of Madaba, the Mosaic of the Lower Baptistry Chapel.
