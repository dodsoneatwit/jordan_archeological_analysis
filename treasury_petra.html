The Treasury of Petra

This magnificent piece of architecture is a part of what has allowed Petra to become one of the new seven wonders of the world. This structure, standing nearly 40 meters tall and crafted with pink sandstone rock, was created by the Nabateans a little less than two thousand years ago (around 9 B.C.E. – 40 C.E.). Such a sight can be seen once one has traversed through the Siq that appears before them. The Treasury of Petra was created during the initial phases of Petra’s development and was seen as a pillar of might for the Nabatean Kingdom. The Treasury also has its Arabic name of “Khazneh al-Fira’un”, which further translated to “Pharaoh’s Treasury: al-Khazneh”. The story behind this name is that the Bedouin’s believed that the Egyptian pharaoh hid an urn of treasure at the top of the Treasury. Moreover, this urn is visibly seen at the top of the building and the Bedouins would shoot at it in hopes of gold pieces falling from it.

Al Khazneh - The Treasury, Petra by Raiz on Sketchfab

When it comes decorations, the treasury does not disappoint. T are numerous key figures that stand out. For one, directly underneath the urn in the middle is a statue of the Egyptian goddess, Isis, and would also be seen as the goddess al-Uzza for the Nabateans. On the right and left sides of the urn are two giant eagles that symbolized Dushara, the Nabateans’ chief male deity. In addition, two Winged Victories (named after the goddess of victory who was also called Nike of Samothrace) can be seen to the left and right the statue of Isis. Between the midpoint and the bottom along the edge are also two lions that symbolize the Nabatean goddess al-Uzza ad towards the bottom are two carvings of mounted riders who were also Zeus’ sons: Castor and Pollux; the remaining carvings are still yet be deduced. The building’s façade appears with about twenty pillars, six on the bottom and fourteen around the midpoint. The thing about the bottom pillars is that none of them support the building itself. Since there is no structural support, an inference has been made that this building was sculpted from where it stands. This art is called “situ”, meaning “in it’s original place”. Moreover, although this isn’t common, the entire façade of the treasury was sculpted from unshaped rock from the original location of where it stands now. For the interior, access to it is restricted but one can manage to peek in a little from outside. There’s not too much to go off though. Small squared blank rooms are seen inside but till this day, no one knows what the purpose of this treasury was. Some say that it was for worshipping but there is not clear evidence that shows that as of now.
