Excavating the Ethics

The deep history and background of the Jordan region is entirely related to ethics, especially meta-ethics. For a quick recap of Jordan’s history, the region dates back to the Paleolithic Age where individuals were hunter-gatherers. This evolved into the Neolithic, Bronze, and Iron ages where Jordanians evolved economically and intellectually. Even after those ages, the region hasn’t stopped growing and in the present, Jordan has become a renowned and respected independent country that many around the world know of. While it seems like Jordan had everything going for them, that isn’t the case.

In 2100 B.C.E., Jordan was struck by a group of invaders that destroyed many settlements, cities, and towns. Their conquer was the beginning of something that prevented Jordanians from becoming independent again until after 1946 C.E. When the Hyksos, Alexander the Great, the Sea People, the British Empire, and many more took control of Jordan, did they ever think twice about how their people felt? This is where meta-ethics comes in. While ethics focuses on what is decided as right and wrong, meta-ethics focuses the nature and origin of morals; it describes that whether something is right or wrong depends on an individual’s reason for their moral beliefs. In the Jordanians case, was struggle for power by many nations for thousands of years actually a bad thing, or did it result in some good? The thing is, after the first initial invasion on Jordan, every other empire that conquered Jordan has made a tremendous positive impact on the region. Now, this is not to say that each one was perfect.

Some might have struggled with maintaining the region for some time but they each brought something to Jordan that benefitted the region. For instance, the Hyksos defeated the first invaders which brought the Jordanians ease of mind while also introducing new ways of transportation, aqueducts, and an improvement in defense around the region. Next, the Egyptians brought new ways of communication, the Sea People brought the use of metals, the Assyrians improved the knowledge metallurgy, Alexander the Great gave rise to the Nabateans and the construction of Petra, and more from many others. From this, it almost seems as though every empire was fair at the end of the day and maybe they were, but one can’t forget that Jordan was originally invaded to start all of this. Moreover, Jordan was conquered countless times for a desire for power that none of the people of the region had asked for. In ethics, one must consider things from multiple viewpoints. In this case, how should believe that the Jordanians felt.

When put in their shoes, it must be terrifying when a new empire takes over. When that happens, one can only think about how not only their lives could are in jeopardy but their loved ones too. In addition, these new empires change a lot that may not have been wanted. These include a change in culture and the way of life of citizens which can put an even greater emotional strain on individuals of Jordan. The fear of knowing that how one functions every day, their beliefs, and safety can change in an instant is very frightening. From this standpoint, this may seem like an example of subjectivism since one has to put themselves in their shoes in order to grasp how the people of Jordan felt, but when you think about it, this is all true and can even be called objectivism since it is factually true that the nation did improve as whole due to these empires. Moreover, we must realize that Jordan is a community of people, no matter who ruled during a certain period of time. The people of the region may have felt a deep sense of emotion for thousands of years, but they also benefitted greatly from these experiences in other ways.

When realizing this, could we say that way many empires did to Jordan was wrong? Is conquering a group of individuals to bow down to your ruling a terrible thing if the people earn from it? This comes down to intersubjectivism which allows the community to decide collectively whether something is good or bad. Its hard to tell whether the Jordanians approved that the results of countless conquering’s were just after being ruled over for thousands of years. Was this because they were fine with this, or because they didn’t have the power to fight back? They gained independence in 1949 C.E., proving that they wanted to choose their rights and have their own identity, which is a fact, but it’s still hard to say whether there historical background was a war within, or an example of a positive impact for many. From what one see, the people of Jordan thought of these rulings as neither good or bad, but as a country, they there was definitely a certain level of acceptance that allowed this to be considered right.Nonetheless, although it’s not morally right to constantly change the way people live as though they were experimental test subjects, in the long run, Jordan benefitted greatly from its historical events, resulting in the prosperous and independent region that it today.
